# Slide Types

Use these patterns to choose slide structure. Do not force all types into one deck.

## title

Purpose: establish topic, position, and tone.

Use:
- One confident title
- One subtitle with the concrete promise or meeting context
- Optional metadata: date, team, version, audience

Avoid:
- More than two headline lines
- Decorative labels that do not add meaning

## toc

Purpose: preview the argument.

Use:
- 3-5 agenda items
- Each item phrased as a claim or decision area
- Optional progress index if the deck is long

Avoid:
- Generic labels such as "背景介绍 / 方案说明 / 总结展望" unless the content is truly simple

## section

Purpose: reset attention between major chapters.

Use:
- One chapter title
- One sentence explaining why the next chapter matters
- A restrained visual signal, such as a number, rule, or full-width band

Avoid:
- Spending a whole slide on decoration only

## content

Purpose: explain one idea clearly.

Use:
- A claim headline
- 3-4 supporting blocks
- Strong hierarchy: headline, key statement, evidence, implication

Avoid:
- Bullet lists longer than five items
- Equal visual weight for everything

## compare

Purpose: show difference, tradeoff, or before/after.

Use:
- Two to four columns
- Symmetric row labels
- Highlight the recommended side or changed state
- Put the conclusion in the title

Avoid:
- Comparing mismatched categories
- Relying only on color to show the winner

## data

Purpose: make evidence legible.

Use:
- One chart or metric cluster per slide
- Clear unit labels and time ranges
- A short takeaway beside or above the chart
- Direct annotation for the important number

Avoid:
- 3D charts
- Tiny legends
- Decorative charts without a source or implication

## image

Purpose: make the subject concrete.

Use:
- Real product, workflow, place, person, or state imagery when available
- One full-bleed or large anchored image
- Caption only if it adds meaning

Avoid:
- Dark blurred stock backgrounds
- Cropping away the inspected object

## ending

Purpose: close with a decision, action, or memorable synthesis.

Use:
- One final statement
- 2-3 next steps, commitments, or decision requests
- Optional contact or appendix cue

Avoid:
- "Thank you" as the only message
- New arguments that should have appeared earlier
